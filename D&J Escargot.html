About Us

Dean and Jessica Dunivan both grew up in the San Fransico Bay Area. They met as roomates in 2011 and soon after started dating. Part of what brought the two of them together is their love for strange and unusual things. Both grew up as animal lovers and dreamed of one day owning their own homestead ranch. Their dream was to become as self sufficient as possible. In order to do this they started to experiment with alternative sources of protien. When they discovered escargot they instantly realized that this was the right path to take. With their relatively small homestead (5 acres) they are able to grow all the food necessary to feed not only them but the snails they are raising also. What started as a hobby has now turned into a profession. They now raise and sale escargot to local restaurants and people across the country


About Our Farm

Dunivan Family Farm was first established in 2022 in Milwaukie, Oregon. What started as a hobby of love has now blossomed into a family business that we are very passionate about.


Reasons Why You Should Be Eating Escargot

In comparison to macro-livestock such as cows, pigs, and poultry, snails require fewer resources to grow and produce the same amount of nutrient rich meat. The carbon footprint of snail meat is estimated to be around 0.7 kgs per 1 kg of edible of meat.

Virtually fat-free, carbohydrate-free and sugar-free, escargot are an excellent source of lean protein

They're rich in iron, magnesium, selenium, phosphorus and potassium

Snails are a good source of tryptophan, an amino acid that helps the brain produce serotonin


Snail Fun Facts

  • It is believed that snails for eating were brought to the United States (California) in the 1850’s.

  • The French consume 40,000 metric tons of snails each year.

  • May 24th is National Escargot Day.

  • Heliculture is the science of growing snails for food.

  • Snails have been eaten as food since at least ancient Roman times. Apicius, the author of the oldest surviving cookbook (1st century B.C – 2 century A.D.) has a recipe for snails in his cookbook.

  • Snails are almost blind, and they don’t have any aural systems meaning that they can’t hear anything.

  • American restaurants serve about 1 billion snails annually.

  • Like most molluscs, escargot is high in protein and low in fat content (if cooked without butter). It is estimated that escargot is 15% protein, 2.4% fat and about 80% water.

  • Even the lazy sloth and the lumbering tortoise have nothing on the snail’s complete lack of speed. Some snails clock in at 0.0004 miles an hour.

  • Research has shown that some snail slime can be used to treat stomach ulcers.
